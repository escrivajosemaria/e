Overview

Community outreach plays a critical role in bridging the gap between health systems and the populations they serve. Many preventable health challenges persist not because services do not exist, but because communities face barriers related to access, awareness, trust, and social norms. Effective outreach brings health information and services closer to people, strengthens health-seeking behaviour, and ensures that interventions are responsive to local realities rather than delivered in isolation. The World Health Organization emphasizes that community engagement is essential for improving health outcomes, strengthening service uptake, and building resilient health systems.

Youth health is a particularly important focus. Adolescents represent over 1.2 billion people globally, navigating rapid physical, emotional, and social development that makes them especially vulnerable to preventable health risks. According to the World Health Organization, many leading causes of illness and death among adolescents are preventable through timely information, supportive environments, and accessible services.

Where It All Began:
Street Outreach in Nairobi

My first deep exposure to mentorship did not happen in a classroom or a conference room. It happened on the streets of Nairobi, working alongside BWAB Kenya. This was my introduction to mentoring children who survive in environments shaped by poverty, displacement, substance use, and social exclusion.

Trust is not given because you have a badge or a good heart. It is earned slowly — sometimes through laughter, sometimes through silence.

Outreach sessions focused on drug use sensitisation, disease prevention, hygiene, and personal safety. But mentorship went beyond information. It meant listening to stories that do not fit into reports, understanding survival behaviours, and realising that resilience often looks nothing like success as defined in textbooks.

Primary School Mentorship:
Foundations Matter

Mentoring primary school children taught me that prevention truly begins early. At this level, mentorship is less about instruction and more about shaping curiosity, confidence, and self-worth. Sessions often blended health education with play, storytelling, and simple reflection exercises.

The biggest challenge was keeping attention longer than ten minutes. That part humbles you fast.

Health topics included hygiene, nutrition, safety, and early awareness of substance use. More importantly, mentorship focused on helping children see themselves as capable of making good decisions, even when their environments are challenging.

According to the World Health Organization, early life interventions strongly influence long-term health behaviours, educational outcomes, and psychosocial development.

Primary-level mentorship programs typically engaged 40–60 learners per cycle, with repeated sessions over school terms to reinforce learning and trust.

High School Mentorship:
Identity, Pressure, and Choice

High school mentorship is where conversations get real, fast. Adolescents are navigating identity, expectations, peer pressure, and uncertainty about the future — often all at once. Sessions focused on mental health awareness, substance use prevention, sexual and reproductive health, and decision-making.

Lecturing does not work. Dialogue does. The best sessions started with one honest question and ended with students teaching each other.

The WHO identifies mental health conditions as one of the leading causes of illness among adolescents globally, emphasising the need for school- and community-based support systems.

Typical high school programs I supported reached 80–120 students per engagement cycle, combining group mentorship with smaller discussion spaces for sensitive topics.

Campus Mentorship:
From Guidance to Partnership

Mentoring university and college students feels less like instruction and more like collaboration. At this level, mentorship shifts toward navigating systems, managing pressure, career uncertainty, and maintaining mental well-being while transitioning into adulthood.

Discussions often included leadership, community service, health advocacy, and balancing ambition with self-care. My approach has always been adaptive, reflective, and grounded in listening.

Mentorship here becomes a shared space of learning — where I learn just as much as I give.

Research consistently shows that mentorship in higher education improves retention, confidence, and long-term professional outcomes. National Institutes of Health ↗

Campus mentorship engagements typically involved 30–50 students per cohort, often supported by peer mentors including James Mwangi, Aisha Noor, Kevin Otieno, and Ruth Wambui.

Digital & Campaign Work

Access Is Not Automatic.
It Is Built.

Young people do not fail to access services because they are uninterested. They fail because systems are complex, intimidating, and often silent in the spaces where youth actually spend their time. One of the most effective access tools is digital engagement — moving faster than misinformation, reaching people where they already are. During a rapid response campaign with UNDP following the Myanmar earthquake, I contributed to online risk communication that prioritised clarity, accessibility, and emotional sensitivity.
