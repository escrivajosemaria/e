Overview

From Field Reality to
Informed Decision

Data collection and reporting are foundational to effective health systems and programs — providing the evidence needed to understand what is working, what is not, and where adjustments are required. Reliable data enables health actors to move beyond assumptions toward informed decision-making that improves service delivery, accountability, and population health outcomes.

Reporting is approached not as a compliance exercise, but as a learning tool. Clear, well-structured reports transform raw data into actionable insights that program teams, managers, and partners can understand and use. By analyzing trends, identifying gaps, and highlighting performance patterns, reporting supports continuous improvement and adaptive management.

Scope of Data Collection

Data collection activities are structured to support Monitoring, Evaluation, Accountability, and Learning processes across the project lifecycle — including routine monitoring data from service delivery points, periodic surveys and assessments, and qualitative data gathered through interviews, focus group discussions, and participatory approaches.

Monitoring Frameworks and Indicators

Frameworks are developed using clear results chains and logical frameworks that link inputs, activities, outputs, outcomes, and impact. Indicators are selected to be SMART and aligned with donor requirements, national health information systems, and global standards such as those outlined by the World Health Organization.

Data Quality Assurance

Data quality assurance measures include standardised tools, routine data verification, consistency checks, supervision support, and periodic data quality assessments — aligning with global MEAL guidance from organisations such as ALNAP.

Approach

The Data Cycle

Data and reporting are approached as integrated components of effective program management. Each stage is designed to ensure evidence is relevant, reliable, and actively used to support decision-making, learning, and accountability.

01

Assess

Understanding data needs, decision-making context, existing systems, and operational realities before any collection begins.

02

Design

Creating indicators, tools, and workflows that are analytically rigorous but feasible under real-world conditions.

03

Implement

Collecting, cleaning, and validating data as a continuous quality management process — not a one-time exercise.

04

Analyse

Generating disaggregated insights and visualizations that explain results, surface equity gaps, and prioritize interpretation over description.

05

Report

Delivering actionable findings with embedded feedback loops — reporting as a decision-support mechanism, not a static document.

STEP 01🔍

Assessment

Examines program objectives, management questions, reporting requirements, and existing information systems to determine what evidence is genuinely required.

STEP 02📐

Design

Balances analytical rigour with feasibility. Data systems perform best when simple, standardized, and embedded within routine service delivery rather than operating as parallel structures.

STEP 03⚙️

Implementation

Data quality challenges often arise from ambiguous definitions, time constraints, and misaligned incentives. Routine validation checks and ongoing support help identify errors early.

STEP 04📊

Analysis

Prioritizes interpretation over description — focusing on trends, disparities, and performance patterns that explain results. Visualization techniques reduce cognitive load.

STEP 05📋

Reporting & Feedback

Reports emphasize implications, risks, and opportunities. Feedback loops are intentionally integrated so findings inform planning, resource allocation, and adjustments.

Applied Projects — Trans-Nzoia County

Evidence in the Field

Beyond frameworks and theory, community health work is grounded in real-world implementation under St. Martha Hospital-supported initiatives. The following project tiles show how data-driven decision-making shaped outcomes in three focus areas.

Malaria Prevention

Mosquito Net Utilisation Campaign

0% lift in consistent net use

Rapid household survey + community dialogue revealed heat discomfort and damaged nets as key barriers. Insights informed targeted health education via CHVs. Follow-up monitoring showed measurable increase in net usage among households with children under five.

Non-Communicable Disease

NCD Awareness & Community Screening

0% lift in screening awareness

Knowledge survey administered at community forums and market-day events. Findings showed general NCD awareness was moderate but understanding of screening importance remained low — guiding simplified health talks and mobile screening days.

Youth Health

Youth Preventive Health Awareness

0% higher uptake via peer-led sessions

Anonymous surveys revealed peer-led and school-linked activities significantly outperformed facility-based messaging. Insights adjusted outreach delivery toward youth participation and localized messaging over generic health promotion materials.
