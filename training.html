Training Approach

A Space for Learning, Mentorship & Professional Growth

Learning is treated as a shared process — where lived experience, field realities, and evidence inform discussion together.

LeafSpire is a youth-centered social impact initiative founded by Escriva Josemaria, focused on mentorship, mental wellbeing, leadership development, and community action. Through structured learning sessions and mentorship engagements, LeafSpire brings together young health professionals, students, early-career practitioners, and experienced health workers around topics that are both technical and career-oriented.

The approach is deliberately interactive rather than lecture-driven. Sessions encourage dialogue, questions, and reflection, allowing participants to engage directly with speakers and with each other. This model aligns with adult learning research, which emphasizes peer exchange and relevance as key drivers of meaningful professional development — National Institutes of Health.

Alongside the on-ground work, the Escriva Hyper Collective YouTube channel serves as a knowledge-sharing platform where training insights, mentorship conversations, and public health discussions are shared openly — extending the reach of learning beyond in-person settings. Global health literature increasingly recognizes sustained knowledge-sharing as an effective mechanism for strengthening workforce capacity — World Health Organization.

03
Frontline Workers

Turning Guidelines into Practice on the Ground

Training frontline health workers consistently reveals the gap between policy guidance and operational reality. Sessions with nurses, community health workers, and facility staff focused on translating national guidelines into practical actions within resource-constrained settings — covering service delivery standards, infection prevention, documentation, and basic data use.

Rather than relying on lecture-based methods, training emphasized case discussions, scenario-based problem solving, and peer reflection — reflecting evidence that experiential learning significantly improves retention and knowledge application in health settings World Health Organization.

20–30
Health workers per facility-based training session
04
Community Level

Building Knowledge Where Services Meet People

Community training sessions strengthened health literacy, disease prevention, and service utilization among community members and local volunteers. Topics included vaccination awareness, malaria prevention, non-communicable disease risk factors, and early care-seeking behaviors. Sessions were participatory — using dialogue, demonstrations, and locally relevant examples.

Evidence shows that informed and engaged communities are more likely to use preventive services and hold health systems accountable UNICEF. Community capacity building plays a critical role particularly in underserved settings.

40–70
Participants per community session, often with local leaders
05
Leadership & Management

Strengthening the People Who Run the Programs

Capacity building extended beyond frontline service delivery to include supervisors, program managers, and coordinators. Sessions focused on leadership, planning, monitoring and evaluation, and the use of data for adaptive management — with emphasis on understanding roles, mandates, and accountability structures within health systems.

Strengthening leadership and management capacity is widely recognized as a key determinant of health system performance. Capable managers significantly improve service efficiency and program sustainability World Bank.

15–25
Participants per cohort — smaller groups for deeper applied planning
06 · What We Learned

Capacity building is not about delivering information

Across facilities, communities, and leadership spaces, training consistently reinforced one lesson: capacity building is about enabling confidence, judgment, and problem-solving within real constraints.

Sustainable impact emerges when people understand not just what to do, but why it matters — and how to adapt it to their context. These experiences continue to shape how every training is designed and delivered.

This reflects a growing consensus in global health that learning systems, not isolated trainings, are what ultimately strengthen health systems over time.

01
Relevance over content
Training lands when it connects directly to the participant's daily reality and challenges.
02
Participation over lecture
People remember what they discuss and apply, not what they are told passively.
03
Follow-up compounds gains
One-off events fade. Reinforcement through supervision and peer check-ins sustains change.
04
Context shapes everything
Guidelines must be interpreted through local resources, culture, and system realities.
