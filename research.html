Anchoring the SDGs in Moral Leadership: East Africa's Development Through Mandela's Lens

This research examines how the Sustainable Development Goals are operationalized across East Africa, focusing on the gap between global development commitments and on-the-ground realities. Using a moral leadership framework inspired by Nelson Mandela, the study assesses progress and systemic constraints across poverty reduction, healthcare access, education, climate resilience, and governance. The project offers a critical evaluation of implementation mechanisms, monitoring practices, and institutional accountability, generating insights aimed at strengthening policy coherence and advancing the region's progress toward the 2030 Agenda.

Unraveling Factors Predisposing Young Adults Aged 18–25 to Mental Health Problems

This publication investigates the structural, social, and psychological factors contributing to mental health challenges among university-aged young adults. Drawing on empirical data, the study explores how academic pressure, social expectations, economic uncertainty, and limited mental health support systems intersect to elevate risks of stress, anxiety, and depression. Beyond diagnosis, the research calls for systemic reform in how institutions address youth mental well-being, positioning mental health as a public health and development priority rather than an individual failing.

Determinants of Maternal Healthcare Services Utilization among Women of Reproductive Age in Gataka, Nkaimurunya Sub-County, Kajiado County, Kenya

This study examines the socio-economic, cultural, and health system factors influencing maternal healthcare utilization among women of reproductive age in a peri-urban Kenyan setting. The research explores barriers to antenatal care attendance, skilled birth delivery, and postnatal services, with particular attention to access, education, household decision-making, and health facility responsiveness. By grounding maternal health outcomes within local context, the study contributes evidence to inform targeted interventions aimed at improving maternal service uptake and reducing preventable maternal morbidity and mortality.

Research Approach

Each stage of the research process is intentionally structured to ensure rigour, relevance, and real-world utility — designed for credible evidence that can be applied to policy decisions, program design, and community-level action.

The overarching goal across all research activities is to produce evidence that is methodologically sound, contextually grounded, and directly applicable to health system improvement. Research is designed to identify gaps in service delivery, understand behavioral and structural drivers of health outcomes, and support evidence-informed decision-making at community, facility, and system levels. Emphasis is placed on practical relevance, ethical rigour, and clear translation of findings into action — reflecting a belief widely supported in applied public health research that evidence achieves impact only when it informs implementation, policy, and learning.

Research combines quantitative and qualitative approaches to capture both measurable outcomes and contextual understanding. Quantitative methods — including structured surveys and secondary data analysis — establish patterns and statistical associations. Qualitative components, such as interviews and focus group discussions, provide depth, nuance, and explanatory power that numbers alone cannot offer. This integration ensures that performance data is complemented by contextual understanding of barriers, enablers, and lived implementation realities.

All research is grounded in ethical responsibility — protecting participant dignity, ensuring informed consent, and maintaining transparency throughout analysis and reporting. Community-based research emphasizes participatory principles wherever feasible, recognizing communities as active contributors rather than passive subjects. Findings are returned to participating communities in accessible formats as a matter of ethical practice, not merely academic courtesy.

Research outputs are designed with translation in mind from the earliest stages. Findings are packaged for multiple audiences — academic, policy, and practitioner — to maximize utilization. The aim is not publication for its own sake but the generation of evidence that decision-makers can act on. This approach aligns with the adaptive programming principles promoted by OECD DAC evaluation standards and the USAID Learning Lab's emphasis on evidence-to-action cycles.

Published research is made available through open-access repositories including Zenodo, ensuring that findings reach practitioners and policymakers who lack institutional journal access — particularly across low- and middle-income country contexts where the research itself is situated. Knowledge sharing is treated as an ethical obligation integral to the research mission, not an optional final step.
